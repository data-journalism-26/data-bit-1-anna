Is Europe Converging? Comparing Living Standards Across the Continent

Comparing living standards across countries is one of the most deceptively difficult tasks in economics. A salary of €2,000 per month means something very different in Bucharest than it does in Copenhagen. Raw numbers — wages, prices, GDP — rarely tell the full story on their own. This tool brings together four key indicators from Eurostat to make those comparisons more transparent.

Source: Eurostat  ·  Data: 2015–2024  ·  EU27 = 100 where indexed

To understand whether people in one country are truly better or worse off than those in another, you need to look at multiple dimensions at once: how much things cost, how much people earn, how fast prices are rising, and how productive the overall economy is. Even then, the picture keeps shifting — a country that looked affordable a decade ago may have caught up considerably, or fallen further behind.

The interactive chart below lets you place any two of four indicators on a scatter plot, compare two different years side by side, and filter to the countries you care about. Blue dots represent the more recent year; faded red dots the earlier year. Lines connecting them show the direction of change.

About the indicators

Indicator 01
Price Level Index (PLI)

Measures how expensive a country is relative to the EU27 average, set to 100. A value of 130 means prices are 30% above the EU average. Calculated by Eurostat using a harmonised basket of comparable goods and services across all member states.

Indicator 02
GDP per Capita (PPS)

Economic output per person, adjusted for price differences using Purchasing Power Standards. The EU27 average equals 100. Removes the distortion caused by comparing nominal figures across economies with very different cost structures.

Indicator 03
Median Income (PPS)

The income of the middle person in a country's income distribution, expressed in purchasing-power-adjusted euros. Unlike average income, the median is not distorted by very high earners and better reflects what a typical household actually takes home.

Indicator 04
HICP Inflation (%)

The Harmonised Index of Consumer Prices tracks the annual change in a standardised basket of consumer goods. Because the methodology is identical across all EU countries, it allows direct comparison of how quickly prices rose or fell in any given year.

What the data reveals

The East–West price gap is narrowing

A decade ago, countries like Bulgaria, Romania and Poland had price levels around 50% below the EU average. That gap has been closing steadily. Compare PLI across years to see how Eastern European economies have been converging toward Western price levels — even as wage convergence has lagged behind.

When GDP lies: the Irish anomaly

Plot GDP against Median Income and look for Ireland. Its GDP per capita (PPS) is among the highest in Europe — but its median income tells a more modest story. Multinational companies book profits in Ireland, inflating GDP far beyond what Irish households actually earn. GDP alone can be a poor proxy for living standards.

The 2022 inflation shock hit unevenly

Set the year range to 2021–2023 and plot HICP against PLI. The Baltic states — Estonia, Latvia and Lithuania — experienced some of the highest inflation rates in the EU, exceeding 15–20% at the peak. Meanwhile, countries like France and Switzerland remained comparatively stable.

Luxembourg: the continent's perennial outlier

Luxembourg consistently appears as an extreme outlier in almost every combination of metrics. It has the highest GDP per capita, among the highest price levels, and the highest median incomes in Europe by a wide margin. Try filtering it out to see how the rest of Europe clusters.
